An “observatory”

  • If we take a look to the definitions
    • “establishment specialising in astronomical, meteorological or volcanic studies”
    • “observation post located generally in a elevated position”
    • “a structure responsible for observing economic, political or social events”
  • Ob7 is another thing !
    • It is a set of resources (human and material) under the umbrella of the French National Research Institute for Sustainable Development
      • Created in 2000 under the name of OSIRIS (“observatoires et systèmes d’information des pêches tropicales”)
      • Became in 2005 “l’Observatoire Thonier”
      • Since 2016 the Ob7 for Exploited Tropical Pelagic Ecosystems Observatory, under the responsability of Julien Lebranchu (julien.lebranchu@ird.fr)

To summarise

And move forward

  • The Exploited Tropical Pelagic Ecosystems Observatory is a set of resources, humans and materials, under the umbrella of the French IRD
  • Its aims are to collecting, storing, processing and sharing data focusing on the French tuna fisheries in the Atlantic and Indian Oceans
  • Furthermore, he provides support and expertise among the RFMOs (Regional Fisheries Management Organisations) and partners (Spanish Institute of Oceanography, Seychelles Fishing Authority, …)

Collection, storage, treatment and sharing of data

  • Each country exploiting fisheries resources must declare its activities
    • What does that mean?
      • Transversal variables: effort, capacity and capture
    • To whom and why?
      • European Commission (if EU member, used for example regarding fisheries agreements) and RFMOs (population dynamics)
  • RFMOs (Regional Fisheries Management Organisations): area and associated species
  • These organisations are composed of the coastal countries of the region concerned and countries with fisheries interests in the region
  • Advisory capacity, but also capacity to define technical measures and obligations
    • Recommendations and resolutions

Rotation around 4 axes

Collection, storage, treatment and sharing of data

  • Collection: recovery of declarative data (logbook for example), sampling, fleet characteristics, etc.
  • Storage: management in databases and administration of these databases
  • Treatment: processes of checking/correcting data to ensure quality data with the least possible bias or specific exploitation
  • Sharing: expertise and coordination at several levels, for research projects, in support of stock assessments, data delivery, etc.

One specific characteristic

Focus on the European Union (EU) in these processes

  • Normal each entity negotiates on its side and is responsible for providing data
  • For the EU member states it coordinates all these tasks
    • Intermediary with RFMOs
    • For France: DGAMPA (“Direction générale des affaires maritimes, de la pêche et de l’aquaculture”) then DGMARE (European Commission Directorate-General Maritime Affairs and Fisheries) then RFMOs
  • The EU guarantees the monitoring of the fishing quota(s) by European fishermen
  • Gives you more impact on the international scene

The Information System

Data administration

  • Administration of a database management system
    • Design and programming operations for the collection, storage, processing and restitution
  • Ensure data quality, identify and correct errors
  • Ensure data security and integrity
  • Ensure that data confidentiality is respected

Data quality

A lot of constraints

  • During the database design
    • Referential Integrity Constraints: ensuring that data is consistent with a reference list
    • Unity constraint: to avoid duplication
    • Type constraints: integer, real,…
  • Pre-checks
    • Check the data before integration in the database (example allow catch declarations only if a fishing trip has been successfully completed)
  • Post-checks
    • Checking the data after entry
    • Limit the length of the sample sizes to realistic values
    • Check the consistency of the fishing season: missing days, same end date between the different sources

Data security

  • Safety aims to protect against catastrophic failures
  • Confidentiality security corresponds to the prevention of unauthorised access or manipulation of information and concerns the fight against intentional faults (viruses, Trojan horses, etc.)
  • For the operating system
    • Fault-tolerant disks: RAID, Redundant Array of Independent Disks, secure data in real time
    • Backup to external media in another location
  • For Database
    • Username/password
    • Definition of read/write access rights
  • For Network
    • VPN (Virtual Private Network)
    • IP-based access control. Encryption of network exchanges

A case study

The onshore data collection

Background

Why we need to collect onshore data?

  • Need of data
    • for compliance (IOTC Res. 15/01 and 15/02)
      • yearly catch data by species
      • Gridded catch data at a resolution 1° by month
    • for scientific purposes (stock assessment)
      • Catches (fishing effort)
      • Biological parameters (biometry, reproduction status, etc.)
  • Provide quality data
    • Data independence: limiting conflict of interest between fishing business and regulation
    • Minimize biases: crew estimations reported in logbook are biased

Sampling schema

Description and design

  • Data collection onshore began in the 60’s and was standardized in the 90’s
  • Objectives: Estimation catch characteristics of the purse seine fisheries
    • Nominal catch by species
    • Catch and effort: catch by species by 1° square/month
    • Size distribution of the catch
  • Target population: major tropical tuna exploited stock by purse and and baitboat fisheries
  • Region: Atlantic and Indian ocean
  • Sample area: ICCAT and IOTC fishing ground
  • Only main ports are covered (Abidjan, Dakara and Victoria)
  • Primary sample unit: landing

Sampling constraints

Onshore VS onboard

Key points for the collect Onshore Onboard
Human cost Sample team at port Observers onboard
Collect location Few number of landing ports Numerous vessels
Sampling period At landing (3-5 days) At each catch (every day during the trip)
Sampling point Well sampling at landing Sampling during the brailing
Sampling easiness Medium (well selection) Hard (fast loading into wells)
Other program link/conflict - Bycatch survey

Purse seine fisheries’ specificities

  • Multispecific catches: major tunas, minor tunas and bycatch
  • Large amount of non-sorted catch
  • Misclassification among species: similarity between YFT and BET juveniles
  • Mix of several sets in wells

A sample = a mix of species and sets in a well

Protocole principle

  • Wells selected with quality criteria (homogeneity in fishing mode and composition spatio-temporal limitation)
  • Identify: 500 fishes for log school and 200 fishes for free school
  • Length measure: Number vary with species composition

Contexte

  • RCG : “Regional Coordination Group”, groupe de coordination régional
  • Objectifs
    • améliorer la coordination, au sens large et au niveau européen, de le collecte de données associé aux pécheries
    • Intermédiaire entre le niveau “national” et “internationnal”
    • Passage d’une dynamique “nationale” à une dynamique “regionale”
  • 6 actuellement en activité
    • RCG NANSEA : Groupe de coordination régionale pour l’Atlantique Nord, la mer du Nord et l’Arctique oriental.
    • RCG Baltic : Groupe de coordination régionale pour la Baltique.
    • RCG Med&BS : Groupe de coordination régionale pour la Méditerranée et la mer Noire.
    • RCG ECON : groupe de coordination régionale sur les questions économiques et groupe pan-régional qui s’occupent de la collecte de données socio-économiques.
    • RCG LP : groupe de coordination régionale pour les grands pélagiques.
    • RCG LDF : groupe de coordination régionale pour la pêche à longue distance.
  • Composé des pays européens concernés par la thématique, des utilisateurs finaux des données et des pays tiers
